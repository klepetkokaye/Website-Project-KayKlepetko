Introduction

  Homestuck, written by Andrew Hussie, is a multimedia work of both literature and art that calls itself a webcomic. It is written unconventionally, using animated gifs and still images alongside what is at times pages and pages of text. It uses a humorous tone that seems to revel in nonsensical comedy, and many of its characters are so long winded that most readers do not finish the extremely long read. However, despite the first impressions that the ‘webcomic’ is nothing more than maybe a concentrated form of internet ‘meme’ culture with the sense of humor of an 8th grader -- crude language and overtly sexual references included -- the comic manages to delve into innumerable themes relating from individual to the multiverse and everything in between with surprising depth and nuance.

  This connects with an article by Aarons Kashtan in which he expresses how in “the post-print age ... the graphic novel helps us reimagine the future of the book”. And while Kashtan’s paper is focused on how the physical book can survive in the digital age, the understanding that the future of the book is changing opens up the possibility that a modality as unique as Homestuck’s is still capable of the same literary depth in themes and ideas as a traditional novel.

  Due to the expanse of themes and the literal size of the comic that I found that I needed to narrow my focus not only to a single theme through the text, but to how only two characters are used to explore said theme. Of the many themes in Homestuck, I decided to focus on the idea of identity. The topic is both one that Hussie explores in depth, and one that is very close to my heart, personally.


Aspect Wheel from Homestuck

   Within Homestuck, identity is defined through one of the 12 Aspects -- Aspects which when combined with one of 12 Classes, make up the ‘God Tiers’ assigned to each of the characters who participate in the ‘game’ that the comic revolves around; this gives a physical representation to the arc the character will undergo. Aspects are different parts of the fundamental makeup of the universe, and Classes reveal how the player is most apt to interact with their Aspect. Identity is connected to the Aspect of Heart, which is defined as “essence of being, of oneself or of others”. In this way Homestuck appears to function under the philosophy of essentialism, “the practice of regarding something ... as having innate existence or universal validity rather than as being a social, ideological, or intellectual construct”

   The two characters I selected for this study are Dave and Dirk Strider. They are both players of the aforementioned game and both of their arcs revolve around questioning this assumption about the essentialism of identity. This brings me to my research question: How does Andrew Hussie use Homestuck to explore the essentialism of identity through Dave and Dirk Strider?
